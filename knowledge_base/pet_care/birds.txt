BIRD CARE GUIDE
===============

FEEDING
The diet depends on species. Parrots and parakeets thrive on a pellet-based diet (60 to 70%) supplemented with fresh fruits, vegetables, and small amounts of seed. Seeds alone are a poor diet for most birds — too high in fat and low in nutrients. Safe foods include leafy greens (kale, spinach), carrots, apples, berries, cooked grains, and legumes. Never feed avocado, onion, garlic, chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, or xylitol to birds. Fresh water should be changed daily. Canaries and finches do better on seed mixes supplemented with greens and egg food.

HOUSING & ENVIRONMENT
Provide the largest cage you can accommodate — birds need space to spread their wings and move freely. Bar spacing must be appropriate for the species (too wide bars are dangerous). Place the cage at eye level or slightly below in a social area of the home, away from drafts, kitchens (fumes are toxic to birds), and direct sunlight for the entire day. Birds need 10 to 12 hours of darkness for healthy sleep. Temperature should stay between 65 and 85°F.

ENRICHMENT & SOCIALIZATION
Birds are highly intelligent and social. Parrots especially require 2 to 4 hours of out-of-cage interaction and play daily. Provide toys that can be shredded, foraging toys, swings, and perches of varying diameters. Rotate toys to prevent boredom. Lack of stimulation causes feather plucking and self-destructive behavior. Talk to your bird, play music, or allow it to watch bird videos for enrichment.

GROOMING
Wing clipping is optional — it prevents escape but limits natural flight. Nails should be trimmed every 1 to 2 months by a vet or experienced groomer. Beak trimming is only needed if the beak becomes overgrown (unusual if proper perches and toys are provided). Bathing frequency varies: most parrots enjoy misting or shallow baths 2 to 3 times per week.

HEALTH & VETERINARY CARE
Birds hide illness well — any sign of illness is serious. Signs of illness include fluffed feathers, lethargy, loss of appetite, abnormal droppings, discharge from nostrils or eyes, difficulty breathing, or sitting on the bottom of the cage. Annual checkups with an avian vet are strongly recommended. Psittacosis (parrot fever) is a bacterial disease that can transmit to humans. Respiratory infections, feather diseases (PBFD), and proventricular dilatation disease (PDD) are common avian conditions.

WHEN TO VISIT AN AVIAN VET IMMEDIATELY
Seek emergency avian vet care for: any difficulty breathing (tail bobbing), loss of balance or falling off perch, severe bleeding, injury, no droppings for more than 24 hours, sudden extreme lethargy, swollen abdomen, or suspected poisoning (Teflon fumes, certain candles, aerosol sprays can kill birds rapidly).

COMMON SPECIES AND THEIR NEEDS
- Budgerigar (Parakeet): Small and hardy, good for beginners, enjoys mirrors and small toys. Lives 5 to 10 years.
- Cockatiel: Friendly and social, crests express mood, needs daily interaction. Lives 15 to 20 years.
- African Grey Parrot: Extremely intelligent, needs extensive mental stimulation, can develop neurotic behaviors without enrichment. Lives 40 to 60 years.
- Amazon Parrot: Loud and energetic, needs 3 to 4 hours of interaction daily. Lives 40 to 70 years.
- Canary: Low maintenance, best for those who want a singing companion without much handling. Lives 10 to 15 years.
- Lovebird: Affectionate in pairs, can be nippy if not socialized, needs daily interaction. Lives 10 to 15 years.
