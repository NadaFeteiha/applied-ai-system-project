RABBIT CARE GUIDE
=================

FEEDING
The foundation of a rabbit's diet is unlimited fresh timothy hay, which should make up 80% of their food intake. Hay is critical for dental health and gut motility. Adult rabbits can have 1 to 2 cups of fresh leafy greens per day per 5 lbs of body weight — safe options include romaine lettuce, cilantro, parsley, basil, kale (in moderation), and arugula. Avoid iceberg lettuce, starchy vegetables, seeds, and sugary treats. Pellets should be limited to 1/4 cup per 5 lbs per day. Fresh water should always be available — a bowl is preferred over a bottle. Never feed chocolate, avocado, onion, garlic, rhubarb, potato, or dairy products to rabbits.

HOUSING
Rabbits need space to run, jump, and play. Minimum enclosure size is 8 square feet with an exercise area of at least 24 square feet. Many owners free-roam rabbits in a rabbit-proofed room or home. Enclosures must have solid flooring (wire floors injure feet). Litter boxes should be placed in corners and filled with paper-based litter topped with hay — rabbits like to eat and eliminate simultaneously. Clean litter boxes daily.

EXERCISE & ENRICHMENT
Rabbits need a minimum of 3 to 4 hours of free exercise per day outside their enclosure. Provide tunnels, cardboard boxes, digging boxes, and chew toys (untreated wood, cardboard). Rabbits are crepuscular — most active at dawn and dusk. Without enrichment, rabbits become bored, destructive, or depressed. Toys that can be rearranged or dismantled are especially enriching.

SOCIAL NEEDS
Rabbits are highly social animals that do best in pairs. A bonded pair of spayed/neutered rabbits keep each other company and are generally calmer and happier. The bonding process takes time and must be done carefully under supervision. Single rabbits require several hours of human interaction daily.

GROOMING
Brush short-haired rabbits once per week; long-haired breeds (Angora, Lionhead) need daily brushing to prevent matting. Rabbits shed heavily 4 times per year — daily brushing during molts prevents dangerous wool block (fur ingestion). Never bathe a rabbit — it causes extreme stress and hypothermia. Trim nails every 4 to 6 weeks. Never cut the quick.

HEALTH & VETERINARY CARE
Spay/neuter is essential — unspayed females have up to 80% chance of uterine cancer by age 5. Annual vet checkups are recommended. Watch for GI stasis (no droppings or gurgling stomach) — this is life-threatening and requires emergency vet care within hours. Dental disease is common because rabbit teeth grow continuously. Ear mites, fleas, and respiratory infections are also common.

WHEN TO VISIT A RABBIT-SAVVY VET IMMEDIATELY
Seek emergency vet care for: no droppings or feces for more than 6 to 12 hours, distended or gurgling abdomen, not eating or drinking for more than 6 hours, head tilt, paralysis or difficulty moving, suspected injury, or labored breathing. GI stasis can be fatal within 24 hours without treatment.

COMMON BREEDS
- Holland Lop: Small, floppy ears, gentle temperament, good for families.
- Mini Rex: Velvety coat, friendly and curious, needs daily interaction.
- Flemish Giant: Very large, calm and docile, needs extra space and food.
- Angora: Long wool coat requires daily grooming, not for beginners.
- Lionhead: Distinctive mane, energetic, needs regular brushing of mane area.
- New Zealand: Medium-large, social, commonly kept as house rabbit.
